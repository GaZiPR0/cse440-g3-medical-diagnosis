# Anemia Explained

**Anemia** is a condition marked by low hemoglobin in red blood cells (RBCs) — the protein responsible for shuttling oxygen to body tissues.

The most common variant, **iron deficiency anemia**, occurs when the body's iron stores run low, since iron is a building block of hemoglobin. Without it, oxygen delivery throughout the body suffers.

This form of anemia often goes unnoticed for years, with symptoms easy to overlook until a cause is found.

Among women of childbearing age, heavy periods or pregnancy are the leading causes of iron loss. Poor diet and intestinal disorders affecting absorption are other contributing factors.

## How Doctors Diagnose It

Diagnosis usually starts with a medical history review and physical exam, followed by lab work:

| Test | Purpose |
|---|---|
| Complete Blood Count (CBC) | Measures hematocrit and hemoglobin levels |
| RBC morphology check | Examines cell size, shape, and color |

Typical hematocrit: 40–52% (men), 35–47% (women). Typical hemoglobin: 14–18 g/dL (men), 12–16 g/dL (women). Ranges can vary slightly between labs.

Follow-up testing — occasionally including a bone marrow sample — may be needed to determine the underlying cause once anemia is confirmed.

## Treatment Paths

### Iron Supplements

Oral iron tablets restore depleted stores. Best absorbed on an empty stomach, though food can help if they cause stomach upset. Treatment may run several months; possible side effects include constipation and dark stools.

### Dietary Adjustments

- Dark leafy greens
- Dried fruit
- Nuts
- Iron-fortified cereal

Pairing iron intake with vitamin C (like citrus juice) improves absorption.

### Treating the Root Cause

If heavy bleeding is behind the deficiency, supplements alone won't fix it — doctors may prescribe birth control to reduce menstrual flow. *In severe cases, a blood transfusion may be required to quickly restore iron and blood volume.*

## Prevention Tips

A diet rich in iron and vitamin C helps prevent deficiency from dietary causes. Infants should receive breast milk or iron-fortified formula.

### Iron-Rich Foods

- Beans
- Pumpkin/squash seeds
- Spinach and other leafy greens
- Raisins and dried fruit
- Eggs
- Clams, sardines, shrimp, oysters
- Iron-fortified cereals

### Vitamin C-Rich Foods

- Oranges, grapefruit, strawberries, kiwi, guava, papaya, pineapple, melon, mango
- Broccoli
- Red and green bell peppers
- Brussels sprouts
- Cauliflower
- Tomatoes
- Leafy greens
