# 🩸 Anemia

> **In short:** Anemia is a condition in which the blood carries too little oxygen because it lacks enough healthy red blood cells or haemoglobin — leaving you tired, weak, and short of breath.

| | |
|---|---|
| **Category** | Blood disorder |
| **Most common type** | Iron deficiency anemia |
| **Key sign** | Fatigue and paleness |
| **Curable?** | Often, once the cause is treated |

---

## Overview

**Anemia** develops when the level of haemoglobin in your red blood cells drops too low. Haemoglobin is the iron-rich protein that carries oxygen from your lungs to the rest of your body, so when it runs short, your tissues don't get all the oxygen they need.

The most common form is **iron deficiency anemia**, which occurs when the body's iron stores run low — since iron is a building block of haemoglobin. This type often develops slowly and can go unnoticed for years, with symptoms easy to overlook until a cause is found. In women of childbearing age, heavy periods and pregnancy are leading causes of iron loss, while poor diet and intestinal absorption problems also contribute.

## Symptoms

- Tiredness and weakness
- Pale skin
- Shortness of breath
- Dizziness or light-headedness
- Cold hands and feet
- Headache
- Brittle nails

Early on, symptoms may be so mild they go unnoticed. They tend to grow more obvious as the anemia deepens.

## How Doctors Diagnose It

Diagnosis usually begins with a review of your medical history and a physical exam, followed by lab work:

| Test | Purpose |
|---|---|
| Complete Blood Count (CBC) | Measures haematocrit and haemoglobin levels |
| Red-cell morphology | Examines the size, shape, and colour of red blood cells |

Typical haematocrit is 40–52% in men and 35–47% in women; typical haemoglobin is 14–18 g/dL in men and 12–16 g/dL in women (ranges vary slightly between labs). Once anemia is confirmed, follow-up testing — occasionally including a bone-marrow sample — may be needed to find the underlying cause.

## Treatment

**Iron supplements** — Oral iron tablets restore depleted stores. They are best absorbed on an empty stomach, though taking them with food can help if they upset your stomach. Treatment may last several months; possible side effects include constipation and darkened stools.

**Dietary adjustments** — Building more iron into your diet supports recovery: dark leafy greens, dried fruit, nuts, and iron-fortified cereal all help. Pairing iron with vitamin C (such as citrus juice) improves how well the body absorbs it.

**Treating the root cause** — If heavy bleeding is behind the deficiency, supplements alone won't fix it; a doctor may prescribe treatment to reduce menstrual flow. In severe cases, a blood transfusion may be needed to quickly restore blood volume and iron.

## Prevention

A diet rich in iron and vitamin C helps prevent deficiency from dietary causes. Infants should receive breast milk or iron-fortified formula.

**Iron-rich foods:** beans, pumpkin/squash seeds, spinach and other leafy greens, raisins and dried fruit, eggs, clams, sardines, shrimp, oysters, and iron-fortified cereals.

**Vitamin C-rich foods:** oranges, grapefruit, strawberries, kiwi, guava, papaya, pineapple, melon, mango, broccoli, bell peppers, Brussels sprouts, cauliflower, tomatoes, and leafy greens.
