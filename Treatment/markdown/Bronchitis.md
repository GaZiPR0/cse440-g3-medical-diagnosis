# 🌬️ Bronchitis

> **In short:** Bronchitis is inflammation of the bronchial tubes that carry air to and from the lungs, leading to a stubborn, mucus-producing cough. It may be short-lived (acute) or long-lasting (chronic).

| | |
|---|---|
| **Category** | Airway inflammation |
| **Contagious?** | The acute (viral) type often is |
| **Main symptom** | Cough with mucus |

## Overview

Bronchitis is an inflammation of the lining of the bronchial tubes — the passages that carry air between your windpipe and your lungs. When these tubes are irritated, they swell and produce extra mucus, which the body tries to clear by coughing. That is why a thickened, sometimes discoloured, cough is the hallmark of bronchitis.

The condition comes in two forms. **Acute bronchitis** is very common and usually follows a cold or other respiratory infection, clearing up within a couple of weeks. **Chronic bronchitis** is a more serious, ongoing irritation of the airways — often caused by smoking — and is one of the conditions grouped under chronic obstructive pulmonary disease (COPD).

## Acute vs. Chronic

**Acute bronchitis** — A short-term infection, usually triggered by a virus after a cold or flu. Also called a "chest cold," it typically improves within 7–10 days, though the cough can linger for weeks. It leaves no lasting damage.

**Chronic bronchitis** — A long-term condition, defined as a mucus-producing cough on most days for at least three months, over two or more years in a row. Most often caused by smoking, it needs ongoing medical care and is a form of COPD.

## Symptoms

Both forms of bronchitis share a similar set of symptoms:

- A cough that brings up clear, white, yellowish-grey, or green mucus (sputum)
- Production of phlegm
- Fatigue
- Shortness of breath
- A slight fever and chills
- Chest discomfort or tightness

With acute bronchitis you may also feel cold-like symptoms such as a mild headache or body aches. These usually ease within about a week, but a nagging cough can persist for several more weeks. With chronic bronchitis, symptoms tend to come and go, worsening during flare-ups — sometimes when a fresh infection lands on top of the ongoing condition.

## Causes

- **Acute bronchitis** is usually caused by viruses — the same ones responsible for colds and flu. Because antibiotics don't work against viruses, they are unhelpful in most cases.
- **Chronic bronchitis** is most commonly caused by cigarette smoking. Long-term exposure to air pollution, dust, or toxic fumes at home or work can also contribute.

## Risk Factors

- **Cigarette smoke** — smoking, or living with a smoker, raises the risk of both acute and chronic bronchitis.
- **Lowered resistance** — a recent illness or a condition that weakens the immune system. Older adults, infants, and young children are especially vulnerable.
- **Workplace irritants** — regular exposure to grains, textiles, or chemical fumes.
- **Acid reflux** — repeated severe heartburn can irritate the throat and airways.

## Diagnosis

In the first few days, bronchitis can be hard to tell apart from a common cold. During the exam, your doctor uses a stethoscope to listen closely to your lungs as you breathe. If needed, they may order:

- **Chest X-ray** — to check whether pneumonia or another condition is causing your cough (especially important for smokers).
- **Sputum test** — the mucus you cough up is examined for bacteria that antibiotics could treat, or for signs of allergies.
- **Pulmonary function test** — you blow into a spirometer, which measures how much air your lungs hold and how fast you can exhale, checking for asthma or emphysema.

## Treatment

Most cases of acute bronchitis clear on their own. The basics of care are simple:

- Rest
- Drink plenty of fluids
- Take over-the-counter pain relief such as ibuprofen to ease aches and cough

Chronic bronchitis symptoms may settle for a time, then return or worsen — particularly with continued exposure to smoke or other triggers. Options that can help include:

- **Cough medicine** — coughing clears mucus, but medicine can bring relief, for example at night.
- **Honey** — a couple of spoonfuls may soothe a cough.
- **Humidifier** — moist air can loosen mucus and ease wheezing.
- **Bronchodilators** — open the airways and help clear mucus.
- **Mucolytics** — thin the mucus so it's easier to cough up.
- **Anti-inflammatory & steroid drugs** — reduce inflammation that can damage tissue.
- **Oxygen therapy** — for severe cases where breathing needs support.

**Behavioural remedies**

- Remove lung irritants — most importantly, stop smoking.
- Exercise to strengthen the chest muscles that aid breathing.
- Improve breathing technique through pulmonary rehabilitation and exercises such as pursed-lip breathing.

> ℹ️ **About antibiotics.** Antibiotics only help when a bacterial infection is confirmed, and can prevent a secondary infection in some cases. They do nothing against viruses. Doctors avoid over-prescribing them because overuse fuels antibiotic resistance, making infections harder to treat later.

## Prevention

To lower your risk of bronchitis:

- **Avoid cigarette smoke** — the single biggest risk factor for chronic bronchitis.
- **Get vaccinated** — a yearly flu jab and, where advised, a pneumonia vaccine reduce infections that lead to bronchitis.
- **Wash your hands** often, and use alcohol-based sanitiser, to avoid viral infections.
- **Wear a mask** when exposed to dust or fumes, or in crowds if you have COPD.

## When to See a Doctor

See your doctor if your cough:

- Lasts more than three weeks
- Keeps you from sleeping
- Comes with a fever above 100.4°F (38°C)
- Produces discoloured mucus or blood
- Is joined by wheezing or shortness of breath

A single bout of bronchitis is rarely serious, but in some people it can lead to pneumonia, and repeated bouts may signal COPD.
