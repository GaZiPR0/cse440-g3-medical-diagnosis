# 🩸 Diabetes

> **In short:** Diabetes is a long-term condition in which blood sugar (glucose) stays too high, because the body either can't make enough insulin or can't use it properly. Careful management keeps it under control.

| | |
|---|---|
| **Category** | Metabolic / endocrine |
| **Core problem** | High blood glucose |
| **Main types** | Type 1, Type 2, gestational |
| **Curable?** | No — but manageable |

---

## Overview & Types

**Diabetes** is a condition in which the level of glucose (sugar) in the blood is too high. Insulin — a hormone made by the pancreas — normally moves glucose from the blood into the body's cells for energy. In diabetes, that system breaks down.

- **Type 1** — the immune system attacks the insulin-making cells, so the body makes little or no insulin. It needs lifelong insulin and can't be prevented.
- **Type 2** — the body becomes resistant to insulin or doesn't make enough. It is strongly linked to lifestyle and is often preventable or manageable.
- **Gestational** — develops during pregnancy and usually resolves after birth, but raises the future risk of type 2 diabetes.
- **Prediabetes** — blood sugar is higher than normal but not yet in the diabetes range; a warning stage that can often be reversed.

## Symptoms

- Increased thirst and frequent urination
- Extreme hunger
- Unexplained weight loss
- Fatigue
- Blurred vision
- Slow-healing sores

Type 1 tends to appear suddenly, which usually prompts a blood-sugar test. Type 2 and prediabetes develop slowly and may cause no obvious symptoms for years.

## Diagnosis

Because type 2 can be silent, the American Diabetes Association recommends screening for people with a raised BMI plus other risk factors, everyone over 45, women who've had gestational diabetes, and anyone with prediabetes.

| Test | What it shows |
|---|---|
| A1C (glycated haemoglobin) | Average blood sugar over 2–3 months. 6.5%+ = diabetes; 5.7–6.4% = prediabetes; under 5.7% = normal. |
| Fasting blood sugar | Under 100 mg/dL normal; 100–125 prediabetes; 126+ (confirmed) = diabetes. |
| Random blood sugar | 200 mg/dL or higher points to diabetes. |
| Oral glucose tolerance | Blood sugar checked after a sugary drink; 200+ mg/dL at 2 hours = diabetes. |

If type 1 is suspected, a urine test may check for **ketones**, and blood may be tested for **autoantibodies** linked to type 1.

## Treatment

Treatment depends on the type, but usually combines blood-sugar monitoring, healthy eating, and physical activity — with medication or insulin as needed.

**Lifestyle (all types)**

- **Eat well** — build meals around fruit, vegetables, lean protein, and whole grains; cut back on saturated fat, refined carbs, and sweets. A dietitian can help with carb counting.
- **Stay active** — aim for at least 30 minutes of aerobic exercise most days; activity lowers blood sugar and improves how the body responds to insulin.

**Type 1 & insulin** — Type 1 always needs insulin (by injection, pen, or pump), along with frequent blood-sugar checks and carb counting. Insulin comes in rapid-, short-, intermediate-, and long-acting forms, often combined to match the day's needs. It can't be taken as a pill because stomach enzymes would destroy it.

**Type 2 & other medicines** — Type 2 is managed mainly through lifestyle and monitoring, plus medication. **Metformin** is usually the first drug prescribed. Other medicines help the pancreas release more insulin, reduce sugar produced by the liver, or make cells more responsive to insulin. Some people with type 2 also need insulin.

> ℹ️ **Tracking control.** Daily finger-prick tests (or a continuous glucose monitor) show moment-to-moment changes, while regular A1C tests show the bigger picture. Most people aim for an A1C below 7% — ask your doctor about the right target for you.

## Prevention

Type 1 can't be prevented. Many type 2 risk factors, though, can be managed. If you have prediabetes:

- Aim for at least 150 minutes of aerobic activity each week.
- Reduce saturated fat, trans fat, and refined carbs.
- Add more fruit, vegetables, and whole grains.
- Keep portion sizes smaller.
- If overweight, aim to lose around 7% of your body weight.
