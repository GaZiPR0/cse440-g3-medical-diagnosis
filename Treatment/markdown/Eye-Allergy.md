# Eye Allergies

Eye allergies, also called "allergic conjunctivitis," happen for the same reason as other allergic reactions: the immune system, which is supposed to protect the body, overreacts to something harmless.

In someone with allergies, the body treats harmless substances — like pollen, dust mites, mold, or pet dander — as if they were dangerous. It responds by releasing histamine, a chemical that leads to swelling and inflammation. As a result, the blood vessels in the eyes swell up, and the eyes turn red, watery, and itchy.

Common triggers for eye allergies include:

    1. **Pollen from grass, weeds, and trees**. This is the most common trigger and leads to what's called seasonal allergic conjunctivitis.
    2. **Dust, pet dander, and other allergens found indoors**. These cause year-round symptoms, known as chronic or perennial conjunctivitis.
    3. **Cosmetics, perfume, and other chemical products** can lead to a type of eye allergy called contact conjunctivitis.
    4. **A reaction to contact lenses**, known as giant papillary conjunctivitis, can cause small bumps to form on the inside of the eyelid. This makes the eyes red and sensitive, whether or not you're wearing your lenses at the time.


## Treating Eye Allergies

Many of the medications used for nasal allergies also help with eye allergies. Over-the-counter eye drops and pills can offer fast relief from symptoms.


**Other Ways to Reduce Symptoms**

    1. Put on sunglasses whenever you go outdoors. They help keep pollen and other outdoor allergens away from your eyes.
    2. Flush your eyes with preservative-free saline solution, or hold a cool, damp washcloth over them.
    3. Use artificial tears (lubricating eye drops) to keep your eyes moist and help flush out allergens.
    4. Remove your contact lenses.
    5. Try not to rub your eyes, even if the itching is intense. Rubbing only makes the irritation worse.

Contact your doctor without delay if you experience intense eye pain or any loss of vision.
