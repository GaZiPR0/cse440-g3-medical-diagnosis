# 🫁 Asthma

> **In short:** Asthma is a long-term condition in which the airways become inflamed and narrowed, making breathing difficult. It can't be cured, but with the right plan most people keep it well under control.

| **Contagious?** | No |
| **Commonly affects** | All ages (often begins in childhood) |

## Overview

Asthma is a long-term (chronic) condition that affects the airways — the branching tubes that carry air in and out of your lungs. In someone with asthma, these airways are unusually sensitive. When they meet certain triggers, they react by becoming inflamed and narrowing, so air can no longer move freely.

The result is recurring episodes of **wheezing, breathlessness, chest tightness, and coughing**, often called asthma attacks or flare-ups. Symptoms range from mild and occasional to severe and life-threatening, and they can change over a person's lifetime.

Although there is no cure, modern treatment allows the large majority of people with asthma to lead a full, active life with few or no symptoms.

## What Happens Inside Your Airways

During a flare-up, three changes happen in the airways at the same time:

- **Muscle tightening (bronchospasm)** — the muscle bands around the airways contract and squeeze them narrower.
- **Inflammation and swelling** — the airway lining becomes red, irritated, and swollen.
- **Excess mucus** — thick mucus is produced and clogs the already-narrowed passages.

Together these shrink the space available for air, producing the whistling wheeze and the feeling of not being able to catch your breath.

## Types of Asthma

Asthma is not a single condition. Identifying your type helps guide the right treatment.

- **Allergic (extrinsic) asthma** — set off by allergens such as pollen, pet dander, dust mites, or mould; often runs alongside eczema and hay fever.
- **Non-allergic (intrinsic) asthma** — triggered by irritants like smoke, cold air, stress, or infections rather than allergens.
- **Occupational asthma** — caused by breathing in fumes, gases, dust, or chemicals at work.
- **Exercise-induced bronchoconstriction (EIB)** — airway narrowing brought on by physical activity, usually worse in cold, dry air.
- **Aspirin-induced asthma** — triggered by aspirin or other NSAIDs, frequently together with nasal polyps.
- **Nocturnal asthma** — symptoms that reliably worsen at night.
- **Cough-variant asthma** — a persistent dry cough as the main or only sign.

## Symptoms

Signs vary from person to person and can come and go. The most common include:

- Shortness of breath
- Wheezing when breathing out (a common sign in children)
- Chest tightness or pain
- A persistent cough
- Trouble sleeping because of coughing, wheezing, or breathlessness
- Coughing or wheezing that gets worse with a cold or flu

Your asthma may be getting worse if symptoms become more frequent or bothersome, if breathing gets harder, or if you need your reliever inhaler more often than usual.

> ⚠️ **Signs of a severe attack — seek emergency help.** Rapidly worsening breathlessness or wheezing, no relief from your reliever inhaler, being too breathless to speak in full sentences, or lips/fingertips turning blue. Call emergency services immediately.

## Causes & Triggers

It is not fully understood why some people develop asthma, but it is thought to come from a mix of inherited (genetic) and environmental factors. Once you have asthma, exposure to certain triggers sets off symptoms. Triggers differ from person to person and may include:

- Airborne allergens — pollen, dust mites, mould spores, pet dander, cockroach waste
- Respiratory infections such as colds and flu
- Physical activity, especially in cold air
- Cold or dry weather
- Smoke, air pollution, and other irritants
- Certain medicines — beta blockers, aspirin, and other NSAIDs
- Strong emotions and stress
- Sulfites and preservatives in some foods and drinks
- Acid reflux (GERD)

## Risk Factors

Several factors can raise your chance of developing asthma:

- A blood relative who has asthma
- Another allergic condition, such as hay fever or atopic dermatitis
- Being overweight
- Smoking or regular exposure to secondhand smoke
- Exposure to exhaust fumes or other pollution
- Workplace exposure to chemicals used in farming, hairdressing, or manufacturing

## Diagnosis

There is no single test for asthma. A doctor reaches a diagnosis by combining your medical history, a physical exam, and tests of how well your lungs work.

**Physical exam & history** — Your doctor listens to your breathing and asks about your symptoms, when they occur, and what seems to set them off, while ruling out other conditions such as a chest infection or chronic obstructive pulmonary disease (COPD).

**Lung-function tests**

- **Spirometry** — measures how much air you can breathe out and how quickly, estimating how narrow your airways are.
- **Peak flow** — a simple handheld meter shows how hard you can blow out; low readings can signal worsening asthma.

These are often repeated after a dose of a bronchodilator (such as albuterol). If your breathing improves noticeably, asthma is likely.

**Additional tests**

- **Methacholine challenge** — inhaling this known trigger causes mild airway narrowing in people with asthma.
- **Nitric oxide (FeNO) test** — higher-than-normal nitric oxide in your breath points to airway inflammation.
- **Imaging** — a chest X-ray or CT scan can reveal other problems affecting breathing.
- **Allergy testing** — skin or blood tests identify allergens that may be triggering symptoms.
- **Sputum eosinophils** — checks for a type of white blood cell linked to asthma inflammation.

## Treatment

Because asthma cannot be cured, the goal of treatment is **control** — preventing attacks and keeping symptoms to a minimum. Your plan depends on your age, your type of asthma, and your triggers.

**Quick-relief (reliever) medicines** — Used only during a flare-up to give fast relief. They include rescue inhalers and nebulisers, bronchodilators that relax the tightened airway muscles, and anti-inflammatory relievers. If someone is having an attack, sit them upright and help them take their reliever — two to six puffs usually eases symptoms. If there is no improvement after 20 minutes and a second dose, seek medical help.

**Long-term control (preventer) medicines** — Taken daily to keep inflammation down and stop symptoms before they start. These include inhaled corticosteroids and, for severe cases, biologic injections. Your doctor adjusts the dose to keep your asthma stable.

**Breathing techniques** — Guided breathing exercises can improve lung capacity and reduce how often severe symptoms occur. A doctor or respiratory therapist can teach these.

> ℹ️ **A note on home remedies.** Complementary approaches (such as caffeine, which acts a little like the asthma drug theophylline, or certain essential oils) may offer mild comfort — but they must never replace prescribed medicine and should never be relied on during an attack. Some strong scents can actually make asthma worse. Always keep a reliever inhaler within reach.

## Prevention & Self-Care

Asthma itself can't be prevented, but attacks often can. You and your doctor can build a personal action plan:

- Take your prescribed medicines exactly as directed.
- Learn your personal triggers and avoid them where you can.
- Get vaccinated against flu and pneumonia.
- Track your breathing and watch for early warning signs.
- Act early — treat a flare-up before it becomes severe.
