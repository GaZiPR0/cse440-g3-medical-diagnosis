👁️ Overview

Conjunctivitis — widely known as pink eye — is an inflammation or infection of the conjunctiva, the thin, transparent membrane that lines the inside of the eyelid and covers the white part of the eyeball. When the tiny blood vessels in this membrane become inflamed, they grow more visible, giving the white of the eye its telltale red or pink tint.

It is most often caused by a viral or bacterial infection, an allergic reaction, or — in newborns — a tear duct that hasn't fully opened. Although it can be irritating and uncomfortable, pink eye rarely affects your sight. Because some forms spread easily, early diagnosis and good hygiene help limit its reach.

🗂️ Types of Conjunctivitis

Viral

The most common type, often from the same viruses as the common cold. Highly contagious; usually clears on its own.

Bacterial

Caused by bacteria such as staphylococci or streptococci. Produces a thick, sticky discharge and is contagious.

Allergic

A reaction to pollen, dust mites, or pet dander. Affects both eyes and is not contagious.

Chemical / irritant

From irritants like pool chlorine, air pollution, or noxious chemicals splashing the eye.

Neonatal

Affects newborns (ophthalmia neonatorum). Can be serious and needs prompt medical care.

🩺 Symptoms

The most common signs of pink eye include:

Redness Itchiness Gritty feeling Discharge / crusting Watery eyes Light sensitivity

Symptoms can affect one or both eyes. A discharge may crust overnight and make the eyelids stick together in the morning. The type often hints at the cause: viral conjunctivitis tends to be watery, bacterial produces a thicker yellow-green discharge, and allergic causes intense itching and watering in both eyes.

🔥 Causes

Conjunctivitis can be brought on by:

  • Viruses
  • Bacteria
  • Allergies (pollen, dust, pet dander)
  • A chemical splash in the eye
  • A foreign object in the eye
  • In newborns, a blocked tear duct

📈 Risk Factors

  • Close contact with someone who has viral or bacterial conjunctivitis
  • Exposure to an allergen you're sensitive to (for the allergic form)
  • Using contact lenses — especially extended-wear lenses or poor lens hygiene

🔍 Diagnosis

In most cases a doctor can diagnose pink eye simply by asking about your symptoms and recent health history — an office visit is often not even needed.

Rarely, a sample of the fluid draining from the eye is sent for laboratory analysis (a culture). This may be done if symptoms are severe, or if the doctor suspects a higher-risk cause such as a foreign body, a serious bacterial infection, or a sexually transmitted infection.

💊 Treatment

Treatment usually focuses on easing symptoms while the condition runs its course.

General care

  • Use artificial tears to soothe the eye.
  • Clean the eyelids gently with a clean, wet cloth.
  • Apply cold or warm compresses several times a day.
  • If you wear contact lenses, stop until treatment is complete, and replace disposable lenses, lens cases, and any eye makeup used before the illness.

Viral conjunctivitis

Since most pink eye is viral, antibiotics won't help and may even do harm. The virus simply needs time — usually up to two or three weeks — to clear. It often starts in one eye and spreads to the other within a few days before improving on its own. If it's caused by the herpes simplex virus, a doctor may prescribe antiviral medication.

Bacterial conjunctivitis

When bacteria are the cause, antibiotic eyedrops or ointment can speed recovery and reduce the risk of spreading it to others.

Allergic conjunctivitis

The doctor may prescribe eyedrops for allergies — antihistamines and mast cell stabilisers to calm the reaction, or anti-inflammatory drops such as decongestants and steroids. Over-the-counter antihistamine drops can also help. Avoiding your known allergens, where possible, reduces the severity of symptoms.

🛡️ Prevention

Good hygiene is the best way to stop pink eye spreading:

  • Don't touch or rub your eyes with your hands.
  • Wash your hands often with soap and warm water.
  • Use a clean towel and washcloth every day.
  • Don't share towels, pillowcases, cosmetics, or eye drops.
  • Change your pillowcase frequently.
  • Throw away old eye cosmetics such as mascara.
  • Never share contact lenses or eye-care items.

⚠️ Complications & When to Seek Help

In both children and adults, pink eye can occasionally inflame the cornea and affect vision. See a doctor promptly if you have:

  • Eye pain
  • A feeling that something is stuck in the eye
  • Blurred vision
  • Sensitivity to light

Prompt evaluation of these warning signs lowers the risk of lasting complications.
